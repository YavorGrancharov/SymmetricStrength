Collapsible Group Item #1
Equalizer is statistic based calculator used to evaluate one's strength balance between certain compound free weight exercises. At the top of all exercises we choose to put conventional deadlift as it is the exercise with highest strength potential over time. All other exercises are connected with coefficients to the deadlift. The coefficients of correlation are developed by observing trainees who had at least six months of strength training experience with a personal trainer, has developed some strength and are familiar with exercises technique requirements. The Equalizer is the only strength balance calculator who is taking height into account. The main idea is for one to know which are the movements that are too strong and which ones need more work, as from one point on your strongest lifts are going to represent not your strength but your imbalances instead. The Equalizer has one major limitation and that is taking only strength into account, but we fixed that by developing skill specific coefficients for each exercise. This way we can differentiate between two people who are training an equal amount of exercises but different types. So if one is doing mostly basic exercises like deadlift, squat, overhead press, and the other one is doing muscle cleans, pull-ups and overhead squats the second one has higher level of athleticism. The system now breaks into two major parts. The first part is Strength Assessment System and the second part is Skill Assessment System. They are separate and yet connected as you can reach your highest strength requirements on your chosen movements but you want be able to reach your highest athletic requirements if don't add all of the exercises involved in the Equalizer. This way the Equalizer can improve ones physical culture by making him involve new exercises in his routine. In the original version of the Equalizer their was no difference between one who is doing five exercises and one who is doing fifteen, and that's not fair as the second one is spreading his effort to a wider range of movements. Now we are giving a bonus first for the amount and second for the types of movements one is doing.
